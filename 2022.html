Honouring the legacy of Sunderlal Bahuguna - Leader of Chipko Movement in India

Albert Camus, a Nobel Prize-winning novelist was once famously said, “life is a sum of your choices”, a sentiment vividly brought to life in the story of Sunderlal Bahuguna, a renowned environmentalist and pioneer of the Chipko movement in India. His demise on May 21, 2021 after battling Covid-19 has dealt a blow to the entire country.

Sunderlal Bahuguna was born in Maroda village in Tehri, Uttarakhand. When he was young, he met Sridev Suman, a famous freedom fighter and inspired by his contributions, became a part of the Independence movement in India after participating in Vinoba Bhave’s Sarvodaya movement. He dedicated his life towards fighting for social causes and writing. A firm beliver in Gandhian principles, he acted as the backbone of Chipko Movement that made well-known contributions towards stopping deforestation activities in the forests. People associated with the movement hugged the trees to prevent them from being axed. This particular movement, which started in the Tehri Garhwal region marked a key event in the history of Indian environmentalism. He then boosted the movement by starting it in Chamoli district of Uttarakhand and then expanding the same to other Himalayan regions such as forests of Advani and Salet in the late 1970s. He is known to have fasted in dense forest areas to mark the protests and thus came to be referred to as the ‘Guard of Himalayas’.

His efforts had such a massive impact that the then Prime Minister of India, Mrs. Indira Gandhi, agreed to accept the valid demands of his movement and banned the unnecessary felling of trees. He was able to establish that rampant deforestation resulted in the erosion of soil in the surrounding areas, thereby leading to natural calamities like landslides. The powerful movement under his leadership eventually drew the attention of the world towards the devastation caused in the Himalayas due to deforestation.

It is known that he never favoured the construction of the Tehri Dam and played a remarkable role protesting against the project for years, holding hunger strikes as a mark of his dissent. He made efforts towards highlighting environmental issues at an international level. He firmly believed that ecology and economy ought to go together hand in hand, and emphasised the importance of such a symbiosis in our lives by raising movements against exploitation of environment. He pointed out that there was an interdependence amongst humans and natural environment, and that our lives are highly impacted and linked with forests, rivers and other elements of nature. He had constantly worked towards empowerment of Dalits and his efforts were awarded when Dalits were finally allowed to enter temples of the Tehri district. He also organised the anti-liquor movement from 1968 that forced the state government to ban the sale of alcohol in 1971.

Sunderlal Bahuguna will be remembered as an inspiring leader and trailblazer for the young environmentalists and social activists. The country mourns the loss of and his simplicity and compassion. His demise has left a void, not only in the field of environmental activism but in the community at large.

About this year’s Event


Legasis celebrates World Environment Day adopting the UNEP theme of
Ecosystem restoration – Reimagine | Recreate |Restore
in association with BSE.

Through the Celebration of World Environment Day, Legasis seeks to insight corporate actions toward Ecosystem Restoration and examine small actions:


a) One micro-ecosystem at a time; Manage your own garden, yard, business premises or school.
b) Start dense urban micro-forests; Greening your home, business, school, or a public space with indigenous trees or plants and make compost.
c) Make Smart Choices by; Halting purchases of products and services that are not certified as sustainable.
d) Raise Your Voice By; Putting on or participating in an exhibition of posters, photos or art showing the beauty of local ecosystems

The economic benefits of such interventions exceed nine times the cost of investment, whereas inaction is at least three times more costly than ecosystem restoration.
